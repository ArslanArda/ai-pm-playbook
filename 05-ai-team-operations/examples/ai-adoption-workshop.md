# Example: AI Adoption Workshop For Non-Technical Colleagues

## Context

A product organization wanted non-technical teams to start using AI tools more practically. Prior attempts had failed because they focused on broad inspiration rather than concrete workflows.

Audience:

- 12 participants
- mix of support, sales, and operations managers
- wide variation in AI familiarity

Workshop goal:

By the end of 90 minutes, every participant should complete one real work task using AI and leave with one specific use case they intend to repeat next week.

## Workshop Design Principles

- task-first, not theory-first
- guided use during the session
- immediate debrief on friction
- no “AI for everything” message

## Agenda

### 0-10 min: Frame The Session

- what the session is and is not
- one sentence on where AI helps most: repetitive drafting, summarization, structuring, first-pass analysis
- one sentence on where caution matters: unsupported facts, sensitive decisions, policy-sensitive communication

### 10-20 min: Show Two Concrete Examples

- rewrite a poor listing description using a structured prompt
- summarize a messy support conversation into next steps

The examples were intentionally ordinary. That reduced intimidation and made the tools feel usable.

### 20-55 min: Hands-On Project Work

Each participant chose one real task:

- draft a support response
- summarize a sales call note
- create a first-pass product brief

Facilitators circulated and pushed participants to:

- provide clearer context
- ask for structured output
- review the output critically instead of accepting it passively

### 55-70 min: Pair Debrief

Participants discussed:

- what worked
- what felt weak
- what task they would try again

### 70-85 min: Group Discussion

The facilitator captured:

- strongest use cases
- common frustrations
- where trust broke
- what training or templates would help next

### 85-90 min: Commitment Close

Each participant named:

- one task they would repeat next week
- one caution they would apply

## Exercises Used

### Exercise 1: Rewrite A Weak Artifact

Input: a low-quality listing description or support reply  
Goal: show how structured prompts can improve a common daily task

### Exercise 2: Summarize Chaos Into Action

Input: messy notes or thread  
Goal: show value in organizing information, not just generating prose

## Facilitation Notes

- do not over-explain model internals
- stop people from using imaginary tasks; insist on real work
- highlight when the tool saved time, but also when review was still required
- normalize the need to iterate prompts

## What Worked

- participants saw immediate relevance because tasks were their own
- skepticism softened once people completed a real deliverable
- friction became concrete and improvable rather than abstract fear

## What Did Not Work Perfectly

- a few participants chose tasks that were too vague at first
- some expected one-shot perfection and needed prompting to iterate
- policy-sensitive teams needed stronger examples of where not to trust output blindly

## Follow-Up Plan

After the session, the organization sent:

- two task templates
- one office-hours slot for follow-up help
- a lightweight feedback form asking what participants repeated and where they got stuck

## Lessons Learned

### 1. Adoption grows from task confidence, not AI enthusiasm

People trust what they have used successfully themselves.

### 2. Workshops should generate repeat behavior, not just curiosity

The closing commitment was small, but it mattered.

### 3. Friction is valuable signal

The workshop surfaced where templates, guardrails, or better prompts were actually needed.

### 4. Non-technical teams do not need a lecture on AI

They need help doing tomorrow’s work a little better.
