# Skill: Set Up AI Team Operations

> **Personalization:** Before starting, paste your completed `my-product/PRODUCT_CONTEXT.md` into the conversation. This skill works without it, but the guidance will be significantly more relevant with your product context loaded.

## TL;DR

Use this skill when your team needs a clearer way to run AI work, not just define AI features. It helps you scope missions, assign decision rights, define review rituals, drive internal adoption, and communicate progress with enough rigor that leadership and cross-functional partners can trust the process.

## When to Use This

- You are starting your team’s first serious AI mission
- AI work feels scattered or demo-led rather than operationally disciplined
- Ownership of prompts, models, evals, or launch decisions is unclear
- You need better weekly review structure for AI quality, cost, and failures
- Leadership wants more reliable updates on AI initiatives
- Internal adoption of AI tools is weaker than expected

## Prerequisites

- A team or initiative that is already exploring or shipping AI work
- At least one concrete AI feature, mission, or workflow in scope
- Rough clarity on who currently participates in product, engineering, data, design, and operations decisions

## Step-by-Step Process

### Step 1: Define The Mission Boundary

- **What to do:** Scope one 4-6 week AI mission around a clear learning or shipping objective.
- **Key questions to answer:** What must be proven or shipped in this cycle? What is explicitly out of scope? What decision should be easier by the end of the mission?
- **Output:** A mission statement with scope and success criteria.
- **Common mistakes:** Framing the mission too broadly; mixing infrastructure and feature goals without prioritization.

### Step 2: Assign Decision Rights

- **What to do:** Clarify who drives and who decides key AI decisions.
- **Key questions to answer:** Who decides the model path? Who owns prompt quality? Who approves eval criteria? Who has veto authority if launch quality is borderline?
- **Output:** A DACI-style decision map.
- **Common mistakes:** Leaving critical AI decisions implicit; assuming technical ownership equals product ownership.

### Step 3: Install Review Rituals

- **What to do:** Define daily, weekly, and cycle-level review rhythms.
- **Key questions to answer:** What should be checked daily? What belongs in the weekly AI review? What data is necessary for end-of-cycle decisions?
- **Output:** A review calendar with agenda and owners.
- **Common mistakes:** Treating AI review as ad hoc debugging; focusing only on build status.

### Step 4: Define Adoption And Change Management

- **What to do:** Decide how internal users or partner teams will learn, try, and trust the AI workflows.
- **Key questions to answer:** Who needs to adopt this? What concrete task will prove value fastest? What training format works for this audience? What resistance is likely?
- **Output:** An adoption plan.
- **Common mistakes:** Assuming a launch announcement creates adoption; over-training before showing real utility.

### Step 5: Set Stakeholder Communication Rules

- **What to do:** Decide how progress, uncertainty, tradeoffs, and setbacks will be communicated.
- **Key questions to answer:** What does leadership care about most: speed, quality, cost, differentiation, risk? How will we explain nonlinear AI progress? What metrics matter for updates?
- **Output:** A stakeholder communication template.
- **Common mistakes:** Reporting only optimistic milestones; hiding uncertainty until late.

### Step 6: Review And Tighten

- **What to do:** Run the operating design against one real initiative and see where ownership, cadence, or reporting is still fuzzy.
- **Key questions to answer:** Where would decisions still stall? Which review ritual would feel redundant? Which adoption assumption looks weakest? What would break first under pressure?
- **Output:** A more realistic operating model.
- **Common mistakes:** Treating the operating design as complete before testing it on a real initiative.

## Decision Framework

| Team symptom | Likely operating fix |
| --- | --- |
| AI work feels scattered | Narrower mission scope |
| Decisions drag or get political | Clearer DACI |
| Quality surprises keep appearing | Stronger PM data rituals |
| Internal usage is low | Project-based adoption plan |
| Leadership updates feel vague | Better stakeholder communication structure |

## Quality Checklist

- [ ] Is there a clear mission boundary for the current cycle?
- [ ] Are decision rights explicit for model, prompt, eval, and launch decisions?
- [ ] Does the team have daily, weekly, and cycle-level review rituals?
- [ ] Are quality and confidence discussed separately from build progress?
- [ ] Is there a concrete plan for internal adoption where needed?
- [ ] Are stakeholder updates tied to evidence and tradeoffs?
- [ ] Is the operating model simple enough to sustain?
- [ ] Has the setup been tested against a real initiative?

## Anti-Patterns

### 1. The Demo-Driven Team

- **Description:** Work is organized around impressive demos rather than learning or launch decisions.
- **Why it happens:** AI progress is easy to oversell through examples.
- **What to do instead:** Run missions with explicit proof points and launch gates.

### 2. The Implicit Ownership Trap

- **Description:** Model, prompt, and eval decisions are “shared,” which means no one knows who decides.
- **Why it happens:** Cross-functional AI work feels collaborative by nature.
- **What to do instead:** Make ownership explicit even when inputs are shared.

### 3. The Build-Progress Illusion

- **Description:** Leadership hears that implementation is on track while quality confidence is still weak.
- **Why it happens:** Traditional delivery reporting habits persist.
- **What to do instead:** Report build status and quality confidence separately.

### 4. The Tool Launch Without Adoption

- **Description:** Internal AI tools are released, but real usage stays low.
- **Why it happens:** Teams assume availability equals behavior change.
- **What to do instead:** Use project-based learning and concrete use cases.

### 5. The Reactive Review Pattern

- **Description:** The team only reviews bad outputs after complaints pile up.
- **Why it happens:** Rituals were never installed.
- **What to do instead:** Create regular PM-facing review loops before launch and after.

## Adaptation Guide

### Small Startup Teams

Keep rituals lightweight, but do not skip mission scope or decision ownership. Small teams can be fast only if ambiguity is controlled.

### Larger Organizations

Add stronger stakeholder communication, governance, and adoption planning. Organizational drag matters more than tool choice here.

### Internal Tools vs. External Features

Internal tools need heavier adoption design. External features need stronger launch confidence and trust communication.
